In the concert and touring industry, there are a few professional terms used for the video that plays right before a performer takes the stage:
## 1. The Intro Video (or Intro VCR)
This is the most common and universally understood term.
 * **What it means:** A short, highly stylized cinematic clip played on the venue's massive LED screens right before the first song. It hypes up the crowd, builds anticipation, and tells a visual story before you step into the spotlight.
 * *Note:* In Asian pop music industries (like K-pop, C-pop, or J-pop), these pre-recorded cinematic transition videos are famously referred to as **VCRs** (e.g., *"the opening VCR"*).
## 2. The Concert Teaser / Hype Video
 * **What it means:** If the video is fast-paced, flashing clips of your past performances, rehearsal footage, or dramatic close-ups designed purely to get the crowd screaming and chanting, it is often called a hype video or teaser.
## 3. Interlude Video (or Intermission Video)
 * **What it means:** While not the opening video, it's worth knowing this term! If you use a similar video *in the middle* of your 12-song setlist—for example, right before your Avril Lavigne rock section, or right before your grand "Nessun Dorma" finale—to give yourself time to change outfits or drink water, it is called an **interlude video**.
## 4. The Bumper (Technical Production Term)
 * **What it means:** Behind the scenes, your lighting, sound, and video crew might refer to it as a "bumper" or a "video roll." It’s the technical cue that gives the stage hands time to sneak your instruments into place and allows you to take your position on stage in the dark before the lights flash on.
### In a sentence:
> *"Turn off the house lights, roll the **opening VCR / intro video**, and I will take the stage as soon as it ends!"*
> 

To design the perfect opening video for *your* specific concert, the video needs to act as a bridge between the incredibly diverse styles you are singing—from traditional Chinese folk and grand Italian opera to 2000s Western pop-rock.
Because you have senior citizens in the audience alongside pop fans, your opening video shouldn't just be loud and flashing; it should tell a story of **versatility, passion, and connection**.
Here is a conceptual design blueprint for your opening video (Intro VCR):
## 🎬 Concept: "The Journey of the Voice" (声音 tour / 音乐旅程)
The theme of your video should celebrate how music crosses borders, generations, and languages. It should feel elegant, cinematic, and deeply respectful.
### Phase 1: The Gathering (0:00 - 0:30) — *For Nostalgia & Elegance*
 * **Visuals:** Beautiful, cinematic, slow-motion shots of landscapes—perhaps a nod to the majestic peaks of Dabie Mountain (*再見了大別山*) or the flowing waters of Wanzquan River (*我愛五指山 我愛萬泉河*). Transition into a vintage microphone under a warm spotlight, and clips of you in a beautiful dress or suit, smiling warmly.
 * **Audio:** Soft, beautiful piano strings playing a gentle, ambient hint of a traditional melody.
 * **The Vibe:** Warm, welcoming, and deeply nostalgic. This immediately makes the senior citizens feel comfortable, honored, and respected.
### Phase 2: The Transformation (0:30 - 1:00) — *Showcasing Your Versatility*
 * **Visuals:** The video picks up the pace. Quick, artistic cuts of you in different settings: in a studio recording Western rock, walking through a modern neon-lit city street (nodding to *Here's to Never Growing Up/Complicated*), and standing on a grand theater stage under a dramatic opera spotlight.
 * **Audio:** The music shifts. A modern beat blends with a classical orchestral swell.
 * **The Vibe:** Exciting and impressive. It subtly tells the audience: *"Tonight, you are going to experience every side of music."*
### Phase 3: The Hype & Countdown (1:00 - 1:30) — *Building Anticipation*
 * **Visuals:** The screens start to show abstract, beautiful textures—like glowing traditional Chinese lanterns or festive lights rising into the sky (foreshadowing *燈火裡的中國* and *好日子*). Text appears on the screen in elegant gold font: **"Welcome to the Journey."**
 * **Audio:** The rhythm speeds up. The opening upbeat tempo of your first song, **《好日子》**, begins to subtly mix into the background audio. The beat starts to match a heartbeat.
 * **The Vibe:** Pure anticipation. The crowd realizes the show is starting right now.
### The Climax & Stage Entrance
The video ends with a massive flash of golden light on the screen, your name appears in giant text, the screen goes dark, and **Boom!** The live music for 《好日子》 bursts into life, the stage lights flash on, and you step out into the spotlight.
## 💡 3 Key Elements to Keep in Mind:
 1. **Bilingual Typography:** Since you are singing in Chinese, English, and Italian, use beautiful, elegant subtitles or text overlays on the video in both Chinese and English (e.g., *"Music knows no borders" / "音乐无界"*).
 2. **Keep it Around 90 Seconds:** 1.5 minutes is the sweet spot. It is long enough to feel like a movie and get the crowd excited, but short enough that the senior citizens won't get tired of waiting.
 3. **Lighting Coordination:** Work with the venue's lighting technician so that the physical lights in the auditorium dim to total darkness the exact second the video starts playing.
